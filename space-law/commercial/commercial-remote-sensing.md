# Commercial Remote Sensing

*Licensing and policy for private Earth remote-sensing space systems under the Land Remote Sensing Policy Act (51 U.S.C. ch. 601-603).*

## Summary

The Land Remote Sensing Policy Act (51 U.S.C. chapters 601–603) establishes a comprehensive federal licensing and policy framework for private Earth remote-sensing space systems. The Secretary of Commerce, in consultation with appropriate federal agencies including the Department of Defense, holds exclusive authority to license private remote sensing operators under 51 U.S.C. § 60121. All persons subject to U.S. jurisdiction or control—including subsidiaries and affiliates—must obtain a license before operating any private remote sensing space system; violation of this requirement is prohibited under § 60122(a).

Licensees must satisfy several binding operational conditions. Under § 60122(b), operations must preserve national security and comply with international obligations. The Secretary possesses broad administrative authority under § 60123 to grant, condition, transfer, or suspend licenses, and may seek judicial injunction to terminate or modify licenses upon substantial licensee non-compliance. Regulatory authority to implement the scheme flows from § 60124, exercised through notice-and-comment rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act.

Parallel statutory provisions establish the government's Landsat program management structure. The Administrator of NASA and the Secretary of Defense jointly manage Landsat under § 60111, with integrated program oversight. Crucially, § 60113 mandates that unenhanced Landsat 7 data be made available to all users at cost-of-fulfillment rates, ensuring U.S. data ownership while maintaining broad civilian, commercial, and foreign access. This dual structure—strict private licensing paired with open public data dissemination—reflects the Act's policy of enabling commercial remote sensing while preserving government data stewardship and national security oversight.
