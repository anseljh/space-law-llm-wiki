# Satellite Communications & Spectrum Licensing

*Communications satellite policy under the COMSAT Act (47 U.S.C. ch. 6) and FCC space-station / earth-station licensing at 47 CFR Part 25.*

## Summary

The Communications Satellite Act (47 U.S.C. ch. 6) establishes the statutory framework for U.S. participation in international satellite communications, while the FCC's Part 25 regulations (47 CFR Part 25) operationalize licensing and technical standards for space stations and earth stations. Under 47 U.S.C. § 701, repealed provisions originally mandated U.S. membership in the International Telecommunications Satellite Organization (Intelsat) to foster a global commercial satellite system. Although much of the COMSAT Act's operative sections are now omitted or repealed, the definitional and coordination provisions remain operative. Section 702 defines a "communications satellite system" as satellites in space relaying telecommunication information between satellite terminal stations, including associated tracking, guidance, control, and command equipment. Section 753 requires federal agencies to coordinate activities ensuring compliance with the subchapter, exercise spectrum and orbital-space authority to achieve coordinated and efficient use, and maintain technical compatibility of systems.

The FCC implements these statutory directives through 47 CFR Part 25, which establishes licensing requirements for space stations (satellites) and earth stations (ground facilities). Part 25 applicants must demonstrate technical compatibility with existing or planned systems, absence of harmful interference, and compliance with international radio regulations and orbital-assignment procedures coordinated through the International Telecommunication Union (ITU). Licensing involves competitive and/or comparative evaluation of applications based on orbital-slot availability, frequency-band assignments, and service areas. Earth-station licensing similarly requires RF-engineering compliance and coordination. The regulatory regime balances private commercial deployment against spectrum scarcity and orbital-resource constraints, with the FCC holding primary authority over domestic space-station and earth-station licensing while coordinating with the Executive Branch on international ITU commitments and spectrum allocation policy.
