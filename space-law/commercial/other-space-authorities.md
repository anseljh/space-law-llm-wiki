## Summary

U.S. space-law administration relies on foundational compliance and documentation standards codified in Title 14 of the Code of Federal Regulations (CFR). Part 402 establishes a unified framework governing documentary integrity across all persons subject to subchapter C of Title 14, which encompasses licensing, authorization, and regulatory oversight of space activities.

The operative legal standards center on three core prohibitions and remedial mechanisms. First, 14 CFR § 402.3 prohibits any person from making or causing fraudulent or intentionally false statements in documents—whether electronic or physical—submitted in connection with acceptances, applications, approvals, authorizations, permits, licenses, waivers, records, reports, or compliance documentation. The definition of "document in any format" expressly includes tangible items such as data plates and marked parts, establishing broad material scope. Second, 14 CFR § 402.5 creates administrative remedies: incorrect statements or material omissions of fact in submitted documents, regardless of intent, may serve as grounds for the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) Administrator to suspend, modify, revoke, rescind, remove, withdraw, or deny any regulatory instrument issued under the cited authority. This standard operates independently of the intent-based fraudulent-statement prohibition, capturing both negligent and deliberate misstatements.

Together, these provisions establish that regulatory compliance in U.S. space law depends not merely on substantive adherence to technical or operational rules, but on the complete accuracy and good faith of all documentary submissions. The regulatory framework imposes strict accountability for documentary veracity as a condition precedent to maintaining authorization to conduct space activities. This structure ensures administrative oversight capacity while preserving both punitive deterrence (criminal fraud exposure) and administrative remedies (license revocation) as enforcement tools.
