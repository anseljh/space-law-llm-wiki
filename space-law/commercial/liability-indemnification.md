# Liability & Indemnification for Space Activities

*Government indemnification, financial responsibility, and cross-waivers for launch/reentry (51 U.S.C. ss 50914-50919), framed by the international Liability Convention.*

## Summary

The domestic U.S. liability regime for licensed space activities (launch and reentry) is codified in 51 U.S.C. §§ 50914–50919 and operates in conjunction with the 1972 Convention on International Liability for Damage Caused by Space Objects, which establishes international state responsibility for national space activities.

Under 51 U.S.C. § 50914, the Secretary of Transportation mandates that licensees maintain insurance or demonstrate financial responsibility covering two categories: (1) third-party claims for death, bodily injury, or property damage arising from licensed activities, and (2) U.S. Government claims for damage to federal property. The Secretary determines minimum coverage amounts based on mission-specific risk assessment.

Section 50915 establishes a federal backstop: when valid third-party claims exceed the required insurance threshold but do not exceed $1.5 billion (adjusted for post-1989 inflation), the U.S. Government pays the excess. This tiered approach allocates initial risk to the licensee while capping federal exposure and ensuring claimants receive full compensation without creating unlimited government liability.

Section 50917 imposes enforcement authority on the Secretary to investigate violations and inspect facilities. Section 50918 mandates inter-agency consultation with Defense (national security) and State (foreign policy) departments. Section 50919 clarifies that space licensing operates independently of other federal permit regimes, except where explicitly cross-referenced (FCC, Commerce).

This framework implements U.S. obligations under the Liability Convention by ensuring the United States can satisfy international third-party claims while distributing domestic risk rationally between private operators and the federal government.
