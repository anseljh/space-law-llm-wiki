# Spaceports & Launch Site Operations

*Licensing and operation of launch and reentry sites (spaceports), including 14 CFR Part 420 (launch site) and Part 433 (reentry site).*

## Summary

The Secretary of Transportation, acting through the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) within the Office of Commercial Space Transportation, is the governing authority for licensing and regulating launch and reentry sites under 51 U.S.C. § 51501. The FAA maintains a dedicated Office of Spaceports to centralize policy development and support licensing activities.

Launch site licensing is governed by 14 CFR Part 420, which prescribes mandatory application requirements, licensing standards, and post-licensing compliance obligations. Part 420 applies an "equivalent level of safety" framework, meaning requirements may be waived if demonstrated equivalent safety is achieved through alternative means.

The licensing process requires applicants to submit detailed information under § 420.15, including operator identification, site location, downrange equipment, and facility layout. The FAA issues a license under § 420.17 upon determining that the application meets information requirements, environmental review is complete under NEPA, and the site location satisfies several protective criteria.

Location approval depends on three integrated analyses. Section 420.19 mandates that proposed launch points be capable of safely launching at least one proposed vehicle type, with "safe" defined as not exceeding a specified acceptable risk level. Section 420.21 establishes minimum distance requirements between launch points and site boundaries based on the debris dispersion radius of the largest vehicle proposed. Section 420.23 requires definition of a flight corridor containing estimated debris from non-nominal flights, while § 420.25 mandates casualty expectation analysis if the corridor contains populated areas, using FAA-specified methodologies.

Reentry site licensing operates under parallel authority in 14 CFR Part 433. The regulatory framework prioritizes public safety through geographic buffering, quantified risk assessment, and environmental compliance, enabling commercial spaceport development within defined safety parameters.
