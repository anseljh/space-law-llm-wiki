# Commercial Space Launch & Reentry Licensing

*FAA/AST licensing of commercial launch and reentry operations under 51 U.S.C. ch. 509 and the streamlined 14 CFR Part 450 framework (and predecessor Parts 400-440).*

## Summary

The Federal Aviation Administration's Office of Commercial Space Transportation (FAA/AST) regulates commercial launch and reentry operations under 51 U.S.C. Chapter 509, which establishes the statutory framework for licensing commercial space transportation. A "commercial provider" is defined as any person providing space transportation services or space-related activities where primary control is held by non-governmental entities. This definition excludes the vehicle itself from the definition of "payload"—the cargo transported to, from, or within outer space or on suborbital trajectory.

The operative licensing authority derives from 51 U.S.C. § 50114(c) and related provisions, which vest FAA/AST with jurisdiction to issue launch and reentry licenses. Historically, regulations were codified across 14 CFR Parts 400–440. These have been streamlined into 14 CFR Part 450, which consolidates licensing requirements for launch and reentry operations under a unified framework.

The statutory and regulatory standards require applicants to demonstrate compliance with safety, environmental, and national security requirements. The FAA must issue a license if the applicant meets technical and financial responsibility standards and complies with environmental review obligations under the National Environmental Policy Act. Licenses are conditioned on adherence to payload review procedures, particularly regarding national security and foreign policy implications.

The framework reflects Congressional policy favoring commercial competition and market-based space development (51 U.S.C. § 50111), while preserving federal oversight for safety, liability, and security. The Federal Government itself must procure space transportation from U.S. commercial providers absent statutory exceptions (51 U.S.C. § 50131), creating demand that supports the commercial licensing regime.
