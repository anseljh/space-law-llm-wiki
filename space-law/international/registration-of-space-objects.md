# Registration of Space Objects

*International obligations to register objects launched into outer space under the Registration Convention.*

## Summary

The Convention on Registration of Objects Launched into Outer Space (Registration Convention), adopted by the UN General Assembly on 12 November 1974 and entered into force in 1976, establishes the international legal framework governing the registration of space objects. Under this multilateral treaty, each state party retains primary responsibility for registering space objects it launches or procures for launch, creating a decentralized registration system with a central United Nations registry as a secondary mechanism.

The operative standard requires launching states to maintain a national register containing specified information about each space object, including its designation, date and location of launch, basic orbital parameters, and general function. Launching states must furnish this information to the UN Secretary-General, who maintains an international register of space objects serving as the authoritative reference point. A state is defined as a launching state if it launches or procures the launching of an object, creating potential joint responsibility where multiple states participate in a launch.

The Registration Convention operates in conjunction with the Outer Space Treaty and other space law instruments to establish state liability and identification mechanisms. Registration serves critical functions: it identifies responsible parties for space activities and debris, facilitates coordination of space operations, supports implementation of international space law principles, and establishes a basis for asserting jurisdiction and control. The convention does not itself impose operational restrictions but creates transparency obligations that enable other treaty regimes to function effectively.

Entry into force mechanisms and dispute resolution follow standard multilateral treaty procedures. The Registry system remains voluntary for non-parties but creates substantial practical incentives for compliance among spacefaring and space-interested nations, as registration facilitates international coordination and is prerequisite to exercising recognized rights under customary and treaty-based space law.
