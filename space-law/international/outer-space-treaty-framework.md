# Outer Space Treaty & International Framework

*Foundational international space law: the Outer Space Treaty's principles (non-appropriation, peaceful use, state responsibility) and U.S. policy implementing them.*

## Summary

The Outer Space Treaty of 1967 (Treaty on Principles Governing the Activities of States in the Exploration and Use of Outer Space, Including the Moon and Other Celestial Bodies) constitutes the foundational instrument of international space law, establishing binding obligations for signatory states including the United States. The treaty embodies three core principles that structure all subsequent U.S. space law. First, the non-appropriation principle prohibits any state from claiming sovereignty over celestial bodies or their resources, though this does not prevent resource extraction or use. Second, the peaceful-use principle restricts space activities to peaceful purposes and prohibits weapons of mass destruction in orbit or on celestial bodies. Third, the state-responsibility principle holds each signatory state liable for national space activities conducted by governmental or private entities, requiring authorization and continuous supervision of private space operators.

The United States implements these treaty obligations through domestic legislation and executive policy. The Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act (Public Law 114-90, enacted 2015) authorizes private commercial space activities and provides a regulatory framework for space resource extraction consistent with treaty principles. Executive Order 13914 (April 2020) further codifies U.S. policy encouraging international support for space resource recovery and use, affirming that extraction and utilization of space resources does not constitute unlawful appropriation under the treaty's non-appropriation clause, provided activities occur within the bounds of peaceful use and international comity.

This framework creates a unified legal structure: the treaty sets international minimums and prohibitions; U.S. statute provides domestic authorization and regulatory procedures; and executive orders articulate policy positions regarding interpretation and encouragement of specific space activities. State responsibility remains the enforcement mechanism—the United States must maintain regulatory oversight of all authorized private space actors to satisfy its treaty obligations.
